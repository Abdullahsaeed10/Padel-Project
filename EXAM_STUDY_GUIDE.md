# PART B — How the analysis was done (understand this, don't memorize it)

## B1. The dataset — say this first if asked anything about data

- Source: **padelapi.org REST API** (official pro padel data service) with my own API key, **rate-limit-throttled** (10 requests/min), every raw JSON response **archived before transformation** so the pipeline is reproducible.
- **776 completed matches**; for the statistics we use the **763 that finished normally** — 13 retired/walkover matches are excluded because their scores don't reflect sporting outcome. *(This answers "why do I see 776 in one place and 763 in another?")*
- Every proportion in the project carries a **95% Wilson confidence interval**; every claim carries a **two-sided test at alpha = 0.05**.

## B2. The statistical tools — plain-language explanations

**p-value** — "If there were truly no effect, how likely is it to see data at least this extreme by pure chance?" Small p (< 0.05) → the effect is unlikely to be luck. Example to give: momentum has p ≈ 10⁻⁹⁸ — essentially impossible to be chance.

**Two-sided test** — I don't assume the direction of the effect in advance; I test "different from", not "greater than". It's the more conservative, honest choice.

**Wilson confidence interval** — a better way to put error bars on a percentage. The naive (Wald) interval breaks with small samples and extreme percentages — it can go below 0% or above 100%. Wilson stays inside [0,100] and remains accurate for small n. *If he asks "why Wilson?": "Because my subgroups can be small — n=17 for Italy home matches — and Wilson is the standard choice that behaves correctly there."*

**Binomial test** — used when comparing one observed proportion against a fixed reference (usually 50/50). E.g., "favorites win 78.5% — is that different from a coin flip?" → binomial test vs 0.5.

**Chi-square test** — used when comparing proportions **across several groups** at once. E.g., win rate across the four chemistry buckets (1–3, 4–8, 9–15, 16+ matches together): chi-square asks "are these four percentages plausibly the same?" (Answer: no, p < 0.001.)

**t-test (Welch)** — compares the **means of two groups** (e.g., duration of upset matches vs normal matches). Welch's version doesn't assume the two groups have equal variance — again the more conservative choice.

**Logistic regression** — a model that predicts a probability of a binary outcome (team 1 wins / loses) from input features. Mine uses **log of the ranking-points ratio** between the teams. Why log? Because ratios behave multiplicatively — a 2× points advantage should mean the same thing whether it's 2,000 vs 1,000 or 10,000 vs 5,000.

**AUC = 0.88** — "Area Under the ROC Curve": take a random winning team and a random losing team; AUC is the probability the model ranked the winner higher. 0.5 = useless, 1.0 = perfect. 0.88 = strong. *One-sentence answer: "AUC 0.88 means that in 88% of random winner-loser pairs, the model gave the winner the higher win probability."*

**Calibration** — beyond ranking teams correctly, do the predicted probabilities mean what they say? I bucket predictions into deciles and compare predicted vs actual win rate per decile. A calibrated model sits on the y = x diagonal. That's exactly what the calibration lollipop chart shows.

**Elo rating** — a rating system that updates after every match: beat a stronger opponent → gain many points; beat a weaker one → gain few. I start everyone at 1500 and process the season chronologically; each player is rated against the average Elo of the opposing pair. **Zero-sum**: winners gain exactly what losers lose (that's one of my unit tests). Elo built only from 2026 results sometimes disagrees with the official points ranking — that disagreement is itself an insight (over/under-rated players).

**Wilson small-n guard in the app** — any win rate with n < 10 is not displayed as a claim; the app literally says "not enough matches for a reliable estimate". This is the *trustworthy* principle implemented in code.

## B3. Finding by finding — claim, method, and the trap question

**E1. Momentum (the headline).**
Claim: set-1 winners win the match **86.2%** (n=763, Wilson CI [83.6, 88.5], binomial vs 50% p≈10⁻⁹⁸). Stable across gender (men 86.2, women 86.3) and level (P1 86.5, P2 85.8, Major 87.1).
The twist: in matches reaching a 3rd set, the set-1 winner wins the decider only **49.3%** (n=207) — a coin flip.
*Trap question: "Is that causation — does winning set 1 CAUSE winning?" Answer: "No — mostly the better team wins both set 1 and the match. The interesting part is the decider: once the match is 1–1, the set-1 information carries no predictive value anymore. That's a real discovery about how momentum dissipates."*

**E2. Ranking calibration.**
Claim: the team with more combined points wins **78.5%** (n=763, CI [75.4, 81.3]); logistic model on log points-ratio has **AUC 0.88**.
*Say the caveat BEFORE he does:* "Points are one end-of-season snapshot, not the values on each match date — that's look-ahead bias, so 78.5% likely slightly overstates true pre-match predictability. I state this on the slide and under the chart."

**E3. Pair chemistry.**
Claim: 230 distinct pairs; win rate by nth-match-together: **1–3: 39.8% → 4–8: 45.3% → 9–15: 51.9% → 16+: 68.2%** (chi-square p < 0.001).
*Trap question: "Couldn't that just be that good pairs stay together?" Answer: "Yes — survivorship bias, and I annotate it inside the chart itself. Both mechanisms are probably real: pairs improve with experience AND losing pairs split early. Separating them would need a fixed-pair longitudinal design — future work."*

**E4. Upsets.**
Claim: among the 165 matches where both teams have a numeric seed, upsets = **22.4%** (CI [16.7, 29.4], p≈10⁻¹³ vs 50%). Finals ≈ 50/50.
*Trap: "Why are finals 50%?" — "Because by the final, seed 1 plays seed 2 — near-equals — so 'upset' nearly loses meaning. The seeding gap shrinks with each round; that's exactly what the chart shows."* Also note honestly: this excludes qualifiers/wildcards, so it's a seed-selected subsample.

**E5–E6. The insufficient-data verdicts.**
Handedness: hand known for only 6.4% of players / 38.4% of match participants → below my 60% usability threshold → **"insufficient data" — I refuse to test it**. Height: 33.8% coverage → skipped. Age: no significant winner-loser age gap (−0.22 years, p=0.28).
*This is a strength, not a weakness: "I predefined a coverage threshold and reported honestly when data couldn't support an answer."*

**E7. Duration.**
2-set matches average **79 min**, 3-set **135 min**. Upset matches run longer than non-upsets (141 vs 89 min, p=0.024). No duration difference by level (p=0.61 — a null, reported).

**E8. Efficiency / fatigue.**
The literal "0 days rest" question turned out **untestable** — no pair ever plays twice in one day in this format (honest insufficient-data verdict). The real finding: the team that spent **fewer total games in its previous round wins 59.7%** of the next match (n=233, p=0.004). Interpretation: winning efficiently preserves something — energy, form, confidence.

**E9. Home advantage — the proud null.**
In the 109 matches with exactly one home-country team, home teams won **43.1%** (CI [34.2, 52.5], p=0.18) — **no significant home advantage**. Spain alone looks positive (61.8%, n=34) but the subsample is too small to claim.
*Why present a null? "Because trustworthy analysis reports what the data does NOT show. A tool that only ever finds positive effects should not be trusted."*

**E10. Scorelines.**
Most common set: **6-3** (399 times). A 6-0 set appears in **10.6%** of matches. Comeback rate after losing set 1: **13.8%** (the exact complement of E1 — an internal consistency check).

## B4. Cross-cutting questions he may ask

**"Why alpha = 0.05?"** — Convention in applied statistics; I set it before testing, and I report exact p-values so the reader can apply a stricter threshold if they wish.

**"You ran 10 tests — multiple comparisons?"** — Fair point: with 10 tests at 0.05, one false positive is possible. My main findings are far below any correction threshold (p ≈ 10⁻⁹⁸, 10⁻⁵⁹, 10⁻¹³); even Bonferroni-corrected (0.005) they all survive. The borderline ones (upset duration p=0.024) I treat as suggestive, not headline.

**"Is any of this causal?"** — No, and the app never claims so. These are associations with confounds explicitly annotated (survivorship, look-ahead). Causal claims would need experimental or longitudinal designs.

**"How do I know you didn't cherry-pick?"** — The exploration script (`02_Data/eda/exploration.py`) runs all 10 questions in one deterministic pass and writes all results — including the nulls — to `eda_results.json`. The nulls are in the presentation. Cherry-pickers don't show nulls.

**"Why is the personal log still synthetic?"** — The insight engine no longer uses it: all findings come from the real pro dataset. The personal layer is a method demo with a visible disclosure, confidence intervals, and an n<10 guard. Real logging is underway; instruments in 07_Validation.

**"What's the pipeline?"** — Acquisition (`fetch_padelapi.py`: throttled, restartable, raw JSONL archived) → Examination (found the score-format discrepancy vs docs) → Transformation (`build_real_csv.py`, separate auditable step) → Storage (SQLite) → Exploration (`eda/exploration.py`) → Presentation (Insights page).
